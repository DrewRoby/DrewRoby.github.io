A Long And Winding Road

Be warned-- herein is the personal page. I guess it's been an interesting road, now that I reflect on it. Maybe you agree.

"Life is what happens to you while you're busy making other plans." - John Lennon

Highlights: A Timeline

2004
Early Life
Graduated from Westminster Christian Academy in St. Louis, MO. Not yet knowing what career path to pursue, inquired with both the Coast Guard and the Navy. I often think about the decision I made then, the signs I saw, and the ones I missed.
2005
Nuke School
Joined the United States Navy as a nuclear operator. Completed intensive training at Naval Nuclear Power Training Command (NNPTC) in Goose Creek, SC, learning the theory and practice of industrial mechanical components for Machinist's Mate qualifications, before proceeding to learn the theory of nuclear power as practiced in the US Navy.
2006
Theory To Practice
Completed prototype training in Ballston Spa, NY. The theory of operating a nuclear power plant becomes real in the form of the S8G power plant. I climb the cooling towers during a midwatch; I am leery of crow-zilla; I read the Antennas manual a half-dozen times on the john.
2006
Anchors Aweigh
By summer I know my destination: Guam. In September, I first cross the gangplank of the USS Houston (SSN-713), where I'll serve under and beside a number of men who are now legendary in my mind. I will stay here until I leave the Navy in 2010.
2011
Academic Exploration
I return to Missouri and enroll at Truman State University, initially studying Computer Science. I join the agricultural fraternity, Alpha Gamma Rho, and switch to Agricultural Science the following semester.
2012
Finding Direction
Unhappy with my career trajectory, I leave university to reassess my path. I work delivering pizzas by night, and as a barista by day. Here I meet my future wife, sister of the coffee-shop owner. During this time, I develop a strong interest in economics during a libertarian phase. I come across Milton Friedman and get off the train at the Chicago school. (I admire many Austrians, but could never get into Von Mises and Rothbard). Become a big fan of Alex Tabbarok and Tyler Cowen's Marginal Revolution blog. Decide if I go back to school, it'll be to study Econ.
2015
Starting Steps: State Street
Move to Kansas City with an Econ degree in one hand, and a wife and kid in another. Temporarily fill in as a mutual fund accountant for a multi-billion-dollar bond fund. Was in town when the Royals won the world series.
2016
Hard Reset: Unyson Logistics
Move to St. Louis to land first real data job, acquired (I'm convinced) with the phrase, "I like to tell stories with data." In school I'd been strongly influenced by a professor, Rossen Trendafilov, who introduced me to the tools used for large datasets, specifically R. Until then, the problems around analyzing years-worth of second-to-second data had never occurred to me. Once they had, I would constantly think about how to model data so that it could be presented in a coherent way.
Had several foundational experiences at Unyson. They were in the middle of a huge system-level software cutover, while implementing new business on top of it. A tricky, fast-paced game to play, and not for the faint of heart.
If half the story is the people behind the data, then I found that half the story is sometimes cranky, fallible, confused, or unhelpful. It's important to bring some grace to work.
In fact, the job of BI Analyst is largely the art of giving people what they want, not what they ask for. That may have been the most important lesson of all.
2019
Lane Change: Enterprise Holdings, Inc.
The decree had gone out from Boston when I was at Unyson, that forsooth, "Data Science Is The Future." From speaking with personal contacts, it occurred to me that if those data scientists were going to have anything to do, then some data engineers would need to lay some groundwork for them. So I moved to be closer to data engineering, becoming a software analyst for a streaming data team.
At Enterprise, I worked with some of the most talented engineers I've had the pleasure to know. They taught me most of what I know about Scala and operating with real-time data in a streaming environment. This was also my introduction to Java-like environments, and how setting yourself up to work in one can be quite the chore (hence one reason to containerize).
Great team, 10/10.
2021
Rainbow Road: Spectrum
This was a weird time for everyone, no?
I worked for Spectrum, helping them untangle some old Talend processes, then re-implementing them in an internally-built framework. Despite it only being a temporary position, of all the qualitative things that changed, I finally had accumulated enough XP to cash in a change in job title from "Analyst" to "Engineer." After years of modeling data in my head and planning out applications... I continued to model and plan.
2022
The Big Win: Daugherty
I was pleased as punch to join an outfit legendary in the St. Louis business world. Saw ERP implementations from new perspectives: from a tiny corner of a gargantuan global concern, to being the beating heart of a mid-sized manufacturer.
There was growth, hope, potential! It'd be nothing but up, up, up into the blue sky from here!
2024
CGI
Daugherty Business Solutions is acquired by CGI in a private transaction for an undisclosed amount.

Personal Philosophy

The world is a creation of a loving, holy, just, infinite, and infallible God. Humankind, having sinned once for all in Adam, requires redemption. This was accomplished, once for all, in the death and resurrection of Jesus of Nazareth, the Christ, Son of God and one with the Father. The chief end of man is to glorify God and enjoy Him forever.

Econ is a pretty useful tool for understanding the above. Psychology is another. Education in the physical sciences, and a life lived in practical application thereof, is of some value as well. However, its value is tempered by a set of affinities a given person may have, which may be natural or societal in origin. Which is to say, it's useful to know why the planets move around the sun, why the sky is blue, and what happens when water boils. This is another thing entirely than knowing innately how to apply these facts, or what lessons may be drawn from experiments with them (deductive and inductive reasoning, respectively)
